<span class="section-header">The Most Distant Explosions</span>
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

The remarkable capabilities of JWST have opened a new window for transient astronomy. Recently, I led the spectroscopic analysis ([Siebert et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.19783)) of a <strong style="color:#FF4A5F">high-redshift (z = 2) SN Ia</strong> that our team followed-up with JWST. This SN is an excellent <strong style="color:#3BB9F8">spectral match to normal-SNe Ia</strong>. The high-S/N of its <strong style="color:#FACA1D">NIRSpec spectrum</strong> allows for precise characterization of its spectral features like ejecta velocity and offers a <strong style="color:#FF4A5F">key point of comparison for Roman</strong>, anchoring evolution of SN Ia spectroscopic properties at z > 2.

The recent JADES Transient Survey [DeCoursey et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05060) observed multiple epochs of the same patch of sky to unprecedented depth (~30 mag). Several high-redshift supernovae were discovered in these data allowing for further spectroscopic follow-up. In [Siebert et al. 2024b](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05076)), we identified one of these SNe as a broad-lined Type Ic supernova at z = 2.83. This is the <strong style="color:#FF4A5F">highest redshift stripped-envelope supernova discovered to-date</strong>. The presence of this rare subclass in this small sample could indicate that their rate is enhanced at high-redshift.

<span class="section-header">Building the White Dwarf Supernova Zoo</span>
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

<strong style="color:#FACA1D">Nebular spectroscopy</strong> can reveal much about the progenitor systems and explosion mechanisms of the large diversity of White Dwarf supernovae. Detailed studies of some individual supernovae show <strong style="color:#3BB9F8">evidence for double-detonation explosions, surviving companion stars, and violent mergers</strong> of White Dwarfs (see [Siebert et al. 2020b](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.13793), [Siebert et al. 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.11788), and [Siebert et al. 2024a](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.12449))

<span class="section-header">Improving Cosmological Distance Measurements with Large Spectroscopic Samples</span>
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

<a target="_blank" href="https://msiebert1.github.io/files/hr_vel_evolution_wallspec.pdf">
  <img src="https://msiebert1.github.io/images/hr_vel_evolution_wallspec.png" alt="Velocities" class="thumb" align="right">
</a>

We used Kaepora to investigate the spectra of 126 SNe Ia that have cosmological distance measurements. Using <strong style="color:#FACA1D">composite spectra</strong>, we found evidence that <strong style="color:#3BB9F8">SNe with negative Hubble Residuals</strong> (those with underestimated distances relative to a cosmological model) <strong style="color:#3BB9F8">tend to have higher velocities</strong>. In the figure to the right, we show the velocity evloution of the Si II $\lambda$6355 feature for the positive/negative (blue/red) Hubble Residual samples. See [Siebert et al. 2020a](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.09490).
